North Laurel ends their season as State Quarterfinalists

By Makenzie Freeman
Published: Mar. 20, 2026 at 8:04 PM EDT|Updated: 3 hours ago
LEXINGTON, Ky. (WYMT) - In the UK Healthcare Boys Basketball Sweet 16® quarterfinals, St. Xavier outlasted North Laurel in a nailbiter, 46-44.

The Tigers came out of the gates swinging, taking a 12-4 lead at the end of the first quarter.

“Just an old fashioned rock fight out there,” state St. X Basketball Head Coach Kevin Klein. “We had so much momentum, heck of a first quarter. Hit them in the mouth, held them to four points, had them off balanced.”

North Laurel opened the second frame with a 5-0 run of their own, cutting their deficit to three points.

After trading blows throughout the second quarter, St. X went on an 8-2 run to close out the first half.

Going into the locker rooms, the Tigers led 25-18.

With 5:15 remaining in the third quarter, St. X stretched their cushion to nine following a layup by Jermaine Cameron.

The Jaguars then went on a 12-6 run, after a three by Jordan Rawlings.

Going into the final frame the Tigers led, 35-32.

The Tigers would be the first to score in the fourth quarter after a dunk by Jordan Jackson.

Kaiden Allen then hit one free throw and a three pointer, which cut the Jaguars deficit to one point.

St. X pulled out to a four-point lead once again, following a dunk by Jackson and a free throw by Connor Klein.

The Jaguars did not go down without a fight.

With 17 seconds remaining in the game, Cooper Elza knocked down a triple to cut the Tigers lead to two points.

North Laurel then forced a St. X turnover to get another shot at either tying or winning the game.

Out of a Jaguar timeout, Liberty Signee and KABC 13th Region Player of the year, Reece Davidson got the ball and attacked the paint, however the ball did not bounce his way. North Laurel Boys Basketball Head Coach Nate Valentine described what his thoughts were on the play.

“Just about getting it in our best players hands and letting him go win the game,” stated Valentine. “I mean, He had the option to get down hill, had Kaiden (Allen) in the corner. He had Jordan (Rawlings) on a throwback, Corey (Cunagin) on the dunker, so you know he had options. He’s made the right decision all year long. You just want a basket there, get to the free throw line.”

North Laurel would foul after that, resulting in Jeremiah Jackson knocking down two free throws to seal the game.

The Jaguars competed all night long, however, the eight-point deficit from the first quarter was to much to overcome.

“We won three out of four quarters,” said Valentine. ”We only scored four in the first quarter, and that kind of put us behind. They came out and did a nice job being very very physical early on and kind of took us out of what we wanted to do.”

Reece Davidson finished with a game-high 15 points, followed by Kaiden Allen, who netted 11 points.

Jeremiah Jackson and Jordan Jackson led the Tigers in scoring, both contributing ten points.

North Laurel ends its season with a 29-8 record and as back-to-back State Quarterfinalists.

LEXINGTON, Ky. (LEX 18) — While convenience like quick dining hall meals and food delivery often wins on campus, researchers wanted to know if the recipes students scroll past every day actually make a difference.

To find out, researchers Aurora Occa and Lauren Batey created their own social media-style content.

"So there are a lot of recipes that we can find online on social media. They are one of the most common form of messages that you can find there. But do they actually inspire people to cook and cook healthily?" Aurora Occa said.

Occa, an associate professor of communication, and Batey, a Win Win program coordinator, designed recipes with busy students in mind. They focused on meals that are easy, affordable, and healthy.

"I wrote those recipes for this study and then in the study we presented them as both Instagram reels so those are the short videos and as carousels so a series of images we were kind of testing which format worked better," Lauren Batey said.

"I would say that it's very challenging to cook healthily when you are having a busy lifestyle. There are a lot of things that are, uh, fighting for your attention. So the idea was for us to prepare a recipe that could be easily done in dorms for our students, but it could also still be inviting and of course uh nutritious and healthy," Occa said.

After seeing the content, the researchers say students did not just scroll past the posts. They acted.

"We discovered that 11.2% of our sample actually cooked the sub baked potato and 19.7% cooked um the small bites," Occa said.

The researchers say the key is meeting students where they already are on social media and making healthy cooking feel doable.

"A single exposure to a well-designed message can move the needle and can make a difference and can inspire students to cook nutritious food as long as we focus on making our messages clear, visually engaging, and easy to understand," Batey said.

LOUISVILLE, Ky. — Head's up Kentuckians, a new text message scam impersonating the state transportation agency is going around.

The Kentucky Transportation Cabinet (KYTC) warned on Friday there are messages being received that impersonates the agency, the Office of Highway Safety and driver licensing offices. It will have a suspicious link similar to other state government website addresses.

State officials said the scam text messages warn customers of unpaid traffic tickets or "traffic infractions" and say a "payment is due" and "actions will be taken" if they're not resolved.

KYTC said they are working to get the fraudulent websites taken down.

Here are tips to spot a scam text:

KYTC does not collect traffic ticket fees or notify customers of violations using text messages.
The agency does not refer to itself as 'KDOT' or a 'DMV' in official communications. 
The official website for motor vehicle and driver services is drive.ky.gov. Beware of any subtle changes, like a hyphen, that might change the website domain.
Here are the steps to take if you receive a fraudulent text:

Do not click the link.
When in doubt, reach out to the agency to confirm authenticity before taking any action.
Contact your bank immediately if you have provided any personal information.
Report the message to the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3), or to the Federal Trade Commission, or visit the Kentucky Office of the Attorney General website to follow their guidance concerning potential identity theft.
Forward the suspicious message to 7726 (SPAM) to alert your mobile provider as well.
